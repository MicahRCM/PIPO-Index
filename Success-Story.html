The Latest Success Story: Southern Utah University

Early Fall 2020 and I opened my daily e-mail news summary from the Chronicle of Higher Education to find that someone had finally solved the retention/graduation/persistence problem!  Who was it? What did they do?  Always on the lookout for exemplar schools, I rushed to click and read the story, wondering what combination of interventions the school had put in place that had made the difference.  The answer, according to the article at least, was a new peer-advising system.  

Thunk.  That didn’t sound too promising to me.  For one, peer advising systems are not exactly a new idea.  I ran one at Ohio State 30 years ago and the idea had been around a long time before that.  Furthermore, lots of schools have peer-advising systems and so it didn’t seem like this was likely to be a difference maker.  If there had been improvement, I suspected something else was responsible.  I was guessing that there had been some changes in the student body that accounted for the difference, most particularly, in the SAT/ACT scores of the incoming classes.  I decided to investigate.  

The article on the school in question, Southern Utah University, starts by telling the story of a couple of administrators who called students who had left the university trying to understand why they’d departed.  That was worth doing because although SUU had been on a long and relatively steady upward trend in its retention rate, averaging a bit under a point of improvement each year over the past decade, it was still losing around 35% of its students at the end of the first year and way behind its peer institutions.  The problem was even more acute after the second year as only about 40% of its students were graduating in 6 years.   

The problem was perceived and painted as a gen-Z problem, a group who need more outreach than students in the past.  They were not going to say “I’m struggling. Can you help me?” according to the Vice President for Student Affairs.  But students in trouble have always had trouble uttering those words and that this clearly wasn’t a new gen-Z problem for SUU, as their persistence record problems extends back decades. It seemed to me that they maybe they hadn’t gotten the diagnosis right, and so the medicine probably wasn’t going to help.  

Second, the medicine itself seemed implausibly weak.  The program assigned all incoming students to paid peer mentors, 120 students to a mentor.  I hate to get down on these people’s well-intentioned efforts, but an undergraduate student mentor has a case load of 120 students?  Unless the university is abusing them, they probably have 15 hours or so a week to devote to interacting with their mentees.  That’s less than 10 minutes a week per student.  They’d probably spend more time than that just tracking down the student to get in touch.  They also sent text messages.  But a text message having a substantial impact on a student struggling with finances, family problems, or poor academic preparation?  That’s magical thinking.  

If it wasn’t that though, what might be responsible for the improvement?  When we look at the data, we find out that for once, the SAT scores weren’t the primary driver.  There has been slight improvement over the years mentioned in the article (2014-2019), but nowhere near enough to account for the upward trend in their retention rate.   The same thing with the other markers of AQ: High School GPA, % of students in the top ten % of their high school class.  All basically steady and unlikely to have had any marked effect on the persistence of the student body.  Compositional factors also appear to have largely been steady.  

So, what has been changing at SUU?  Well, the first thing to notice is that size of the student population has been growing radically.  Over the relatively short period in question, the size of the first-year class more than doubled!  One way they did this, however, was by lowering the bar for admissions.  At one point in time, it was relatively difficult to get into SUU.  In 2013, the acceptance rate bottomed out at 57%.  This past year is had grown to 79%--4 out of every 5 students who applied to SUU were accepted.  But neither class size nor acceptance rate suggests any particular advantage when it comes to retention (it’s noteworthy, however, that they kept their AQ markers steady while growing so rapidly).  

But there was another change that does matter: the way financial aid was distributed.  In particular, SUU shifted aid toward needy students.  The average aid award to students did not increase, but how it was distributed changed a lot.  For example, in 2014, the percent of student at SUU who had their full need met was very low—only 1.4%.  That meant a lot of students were attending without the resources they really needed to get through school.  In 2019, that number was up to 21.6%!  That is a huge turnaround and meant that a lot more students were actually equipped with the money they needed to keep going at SUU.  

We can see this in a different way when we look at merit-based aid versus need-based aid.  In 2014, the average merit-based scholarship at SUU was more than twice as big as the average need-based award (2.1 times as high, to be exact).  Only one year later, the average need-based scholarship was slightly lower, but the merit-based scholarship had dropped 36%, such that the ratio of merit to need award was about 1.4%.  That approximate ratio has continued since then and no doubt is helping many more of its growing student body get to that “need-met” status.  

So, SUU has done something good and laudable that is beyond PIPO and beyond manipulating composition.  And I congratulate them and their leadership for addressing a critical issue—financial need---that challenges student’s ability to persist in pursuit of their higher education goals.  It is a story of improvement in many ways, and they are definitely making progress. But the struggle is long and tough.  Even with recent improvements, their latest 6-year graduation rate is still just over 40% and their retention rate is approaching 70%.  Both of those values, even though they are improved over the past, are underperforming relative to privilege peers.  I look forward to watching their efforts—and I hope continued progress—over the next few years.
